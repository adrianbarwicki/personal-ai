# Russian / Post-Soviet Cultural Notes

Cultural patterns that catch even Polish guests off-guard when interacting with Russian-speaking (incl. Belarusian) families.

---

## 1. Shoes off, slippers on

The moment you enter — shoes off. They'll have **тапочки** (*tápochki*, slippers) for you. Wear them. Refusing reads as cold.

---

## 2. Don't praise too directly — superstition is real

Pattern around **сглаз** (*sglaz* — "jinxing / evil eye"). Don't praise things you want to keep going:

- **Children** — never "what a beautiful, healthy child." Soften to *"какой хороший"* + sometimes **тьфу-тьфу-тьфу** (mimed spitting over shoulder).
- **Plans / future events** — don't say "I'm sure it'll go great." Say *"дай Бог"* (*daj Bokh* — "God willing") or *"посмотрим"* ("we'll see").
- **Health** — don't say "you look so healthy." Feels like inviting illness.
- **Lia's beauty in front of them** — praise her **character** instead.

Polish culture has a bit of this (*odpukać w niemalowane*); Belarusian/Russian families take it more seriously even when secular.

---

## 3. Religion — read the room

| Possibility | Signals |
|---|---|
| Orthodox Christian | Icons on walls, mentions of *батюшка*, Пасха (Easter) is huge |
| Soviet atheist | No icons, but still culturally celebrate Orthodox Easter |
| Catholic (western Belarus) | Catholic iconography, may be more Polish-friendly |

If they cross themselves before eating — sit quietly, don't eat until they're done. Don't volunteer religious commentary; let it come up naturally.

---

## 4. The Polish dimension (specific to Adrian)

- Most Belarusians in Poland chose Poland deliberately. Generally positive.
- They'll assume you know Slavic basics — shoes off, food = love. Don't over-play the foreigner card.
- Poland-Belarus history has rough patches (Polonization, post-WWII border). Don't bring up. If they bring up, listen.
- Many Belarusians understand Polish. Offer Polish; they'll usually politely decline and stick to Russian.
- Catholic question may come up. Honest, no embellishment.

---

## 5. Money & gifts

- **You pay** at restaurants. Don't split — reads as cold/Western. Fight to pay.
- If they hosted you, return the gesture later with substance.
- Don't discuss income / financials directly with elders.
- Gifts aren't tit-for-tat — they're status signals. Effort > price.

**Gift ideas for visiting their home:**
1. **Flowers for mom** — odd number of stems (even = funerals). Avoid yellow. Roses, tulips, chrysanthemums safe.
2. Cake / pastries from a known bakery in a box.
3. Bottle for dad — wine, cognac (коньяк), or Polish Żubrówka.

Hand flowers to mom first, eye contact: **"Это вам"** (*Éta vam* — "This is for you").

---

## 6. Privacy goes both directions

**They may ask things Westerners find intrusive:**
- How much do you earn?
- Do you own your apartment?
- When are you getting married / having kids?
- How's your health?

Not rude in their culture — it's how parents evaluate a partner. Have light, honest, non-defensive answers. Smile deflection is fine: **"Скоро увидим"** (*skóra uvídim* — "we'll see soon").

**Don't ask them about:**
- Politics, the war, Lukashenko, 2020 protests, KGB
- Why they left Belarus (unless they bring it up)
- Family who stayed behind

---

## 7. Mother-daughter dynamic

In Slavic / post-Soviet families, mother-daughter bond is intense. Mothers see partners as:
- **An ally** who helps care for their daughter (you want this)
- **A threat** taking her away (avoid this)

**Ally signals:**
- Notice things Lia does for the family. *"Лия мне рассказывала, как вы её научили готовить [dish]"*
- Affectionate but **not sexual** in front of parents — hand on back, smile, not kissing at the table.
- Acknowledge she'll always be their daughter: *"я знаю, как она вас любит"*
- Give them time alone with her. Don't tag along if mom calls her to the kitchen.

---

## 8. "Showing face" — dress up

Slavic norms here are stricter than Polish norms.

- Dress one level up from what you'd wear in Poland.
- Clean shoes, ironed shirt, no wrinkled t-shirts.
- Mom will be dressed up. Match the energy.
- Light cologne is fine; heavy fragrance reads as trying too hard.

---

## 9. Don't be too "casual cool"

Things that read as disrespectful to older Slavic eyes:
- Hands in pockets while talking to them
- Slouching at the table
- Phone visible on the table (pocket, silent)
- Calling them "guys" or any casual collective
- Joking about their food, accent, country, age — even gently
- Cursing, even mild, in early visits

Earn casualness over multiple visits.

---

## 10. Time and pace

- Meals are 2-3 hours. No phone. No sighing. No clock-checking.
- Tea after dinner is its own ritual with sweets. Saying no to tea is mildly insulting.
- Leaving takes 30+ minutes. **Долгие проводы** ("long farewells") is a feature, not a bug. Don't rush.

---

## 11. Threshold superstitions

- Don't shake hands or hug across the threshold.
- Don't say goodbye in the doorway.
- If you forget something and have to go back — look in a mirror before leaving again (*Посмотри в зеркало*).
- Don't sit on the threshold to put shoes on.

---

## 12. Compliments that always land

- **"У вас очень уютно"** — "It's very cozy at your place" (any apartment, any decor)
- **"Сразу видно, что вы её хорошо воспитали"** — "You can immediately see you raised her well" → rocket fuel
- **"Спасибо, что приняли меня"** — "Thank you for receiving me"

---

## 13. The one rule that beats everything

**Be visibly, consistently good to their daughter** — not in performance, but in small things they observe:

- Pull out her chair
- Pour her water before yours
- Listen when she speaks; look at her
- If she's helping in the kitchen, help too
- Gently defend her if anyone teases
- When leaving: take care of her coat, bag, open the door

Slavic parents watch *how you treat their daughter* more than what you say. The language stuff opens the door; how you behave with her closes the deal.
