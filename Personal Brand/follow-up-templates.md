# 48h Follow-up Templates — Adrian's Personal LinkedIn

The original post gets ~24h of algorithmic boost. The follow-up post 48h later re-surfaces the original by linking back into its comment thread, and extends the conversation lifecycle by another 2–3 days.

**Skipping the follow-up leaves ~50% of the eventual reach on the table.** Treat it as part of the original post's distribution plan, not a separate post.

## When to post

- **48–60 hours after the original**, not sooner (let comments accumulate) and not later (algorithmic decay starts after 72h)
- Same time-of-day window as the original — feeds the same audience
- Don't post a follow-up if the original got < 20 reactions in 24h — the algorithm has already moved on; better to ship something fresh

## Four templates — pick on the day

### Template A — Reply to a sceptic (highest engagement potential)

Use when someone in comments pushed back hard. Disagreement keeps threads hot longer than agreement.

```
A comment on my [topic] post stuck with me:

"[Paraphrase their pushback in 1–2 lines —
make it sharp, don't strawman]"

I've been thinking about this for two days.

Here's where I think they're partially right:
[concession — 1 line of genuine agreement
acknowledging the half-truth in their point]

And here's where I still disagree:
[your actual counter — with one concrete number
or example that the original post didn't have room for]

[The deeper version of your original take —
a layer below what you posted before]

So my updated position:
[Refined version of the original claim. Keep
the spice; lose any indefensible part.]

What did I miss?
```

*Why this works:* showing public concession ("they're partially right") signals intellectual honesty without abandoning the take. "What did I miss?" re-opens the comment loop.

### Template B — Amplify agreement

Use when the best comment was someone validating with their own example.

```
[Name from comment, no @-tag] said in the comments
that they're seeing the same thing in [their context].

That's the pattern I missed when I posted —
[counter-example or refinement that adds nuance].

The version of my take I'd post now:

[Refined claim. Slightly less spicy, more
data-backed than the original.]

For anyone running this experiment in their own
company — the leading indicator I'd watch:
[concrete metric or signal]

Where are you seeing this break down?
```

*Why this works:* "the version I'd post now" lets you keep evolving the public take. Asking "where does it break down?" surfaces failure cases — best for trust-building.

### Template C — Own the numbers (when challenged on a stat)

Use when someone called out a specific number from the original ("how did you get X%?"). Defending OR refining in public is fine; pretending it didn't happen is the worst option.

```
A few people asked how I got to "[the spicy number]"
in my last post.

Here's the actual case I was thinking of:

[Concrete example. One feature/situation. Round-number
version is fine — be honest about which numbers are
precise vs vibes.]

If I had to publish a defensible average across
[N] cases, the honest number would probably be:
[realistic range, less spicy than the original]

Still a step-change. Still worth restructuring around.
But the original was a punchy approximation, not
a measurement.

For anyone keeping their own scorecard — what's
the actual ratio in your company right now?
```

*Why this works:* "punchy approximation, not a measurement" is the credibility-recovery line. Audiences forgive a spicy round-up if you'll own it; they don't forgive vagueness when pressed.

### Template D — Deepen one point (fallback)

Use when the original got engagement but no quotable comment to build on. Pick the strongest line from the original and go deeper.

```
A follow-up to my [topic] post.

The line that got the most replies was about
[strongest single claim].

A few people asked: why [the specific number/claim]?

Here's the mental model —

[2-3 paragraphs unpacking the claim. New angle
the original didn't have room for. New concrete
example if possible.]

[Single-sentence restatement of the original
claim, now with deeper foundation.]

If you're [reader's situation], that's the thing
to look at first — not [the obvious thing
people focus on], the [deeper structural thing].

[Closing question that invites their case.]
```

*Why this works:* takes the screenshotted line and gives it a deeper foundation. Audiences who quoted the original now have a longer answer to circulate.

## Decision tree — pick the template on the day

```
Did a sceptic show up with a sharp counter?
├── YES → Template A (highest engagement potential)
└── NO  → Did the post hit 40+ reactions?
         ├── YES → Did anyone challenge the numbers?
         │        ├── YES → Template C (own the spicy round-up)
         │        └── NO  → Template B (amplify agreement)
         └── NO  → Template D (deepen one point, salvage reach)
```

## What every follow-up needs regardless of template

- **Reference the original post explicitly** ("a comment on my [topic] post", "a follow-up to my [topic] post") — drives a portion of the audience back to the original, signalling sustained engagement to the algorithm
- **Lead with what's new** — never just summarise the original. The hook in the follow-up has to land independently
- **End with a different question than the original** — keeps the audience from feeling like they already answered this
- **No new hashtags** — they create algorithmic noise around a post that's already finding its audience
- **No screenshots of original-post analytics** — looks defensive

## What kills follow-up reach

- Posting < 24h after the original — competes with itself for the same audience
- Quoting a commenter with @-tag — turns the comment thread into a back-and-forth between two people, not a public conversation
- Defensive tone — "as I said in my last post" / "people misunderstood" — comes across as fragile, kills comments
- Posting the follow-up before replying to most of the original's comments — the audience reads it as "moving on" from their input
